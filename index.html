How Training Actually Works
The foundational loop behind all progress
Foundational ▼
The Adaptation Loop

Training works through a three-step loop: Stimulus → Recovery → Adaptation. You apply a stress your body isn't used to, you rest so your body can repair, and your body rebuilds slightly stronger to handle that stress next time.

Skip any step and the loop breaks. Train without enough stress and nothing changes. Train without enough recovery and you break down faster than you build up.

Progressive Overload

Your body adapts to whatever it's consistently exposed to — and once adapted, that same stimulus no longer produces a response. You have to progressively increase the challenge over time. You can progress by adding reps, adding sets, reducing rest, improving technique, or using harder variations and modifiers (pauses, tempo, range of motion).

Why You Can't Always Go Hard

Fatigue is cumulative. Every hard session creates fatigue that sits on top of your fitness. In the short term, fatigue masks your true fitness level. Over a multi-week program, you accumulate fatigue intentionally, then taper off so your fitness is revealed through your peak performance. This is why deload weeks, easy sessions, and sleep all matter — they're part of the loop.

In the app

Weekly Templates let you repeat the same structure each week and manually add load or reps over time. Full Programs automate the overload logic — they rotate stimulus types across blocks, build volume, and include a deload so your body can peak. Both apply this loop; they give you different amounts of control.

The Three-Layer Vocabulary
Goal · Stimulus · Phase — how the app thinks about training
Foundational ▼

The app organises training across three distinct layers. Understanding these makes every screen make sense.

Goal Template-level. The primary adaptation you are chasing across the week: Strength, Hypertrophy, Power, or Conditioning. A template has one goal. Every session in that template serves it.
Stimulus Day-level. The specific physiological demand of a single training day — more precise than goal. Includes MaxForce, MuscleGrowth, AerobicBase, Threshold, VO2Max, Anaerobic, ExplosivePower, and PowerSupport. A hybrid day can carry two stimuli (e.g. MaxForce + AerobicBase).
Phase Program-level. Where a training block sits in the periodization arc: Accumulation (build volume), Intensification (increase load), Realization (peak), or Deload (recover). Phases span multiple weeks and exist inside Full Programs only.
In the app

In the Plan tab, template cards are filtered by Goal. Each day row inside a card shows its Stimulus badges. In Full Programs, the block timeline shows Phase chips. These three layers never collide — Goal describes the week, Stimulus describes the day, Phase describes the arc.

The Eight Stimulus Types
What each training day is actually trying to do
Foundational ▼

Every training day is tagged with a stimulus — the specific adaptation it's targeting. Slot rules and exercise selection are both governed by this tag.

Strength & Power Stimuli
MaxForce Maximal strength — teaching the nervous system to recruit maximum motor units. 85–95% 1RM, 1–5 reps, 3–5 min rest. Every rep is deliberate and near-limit.
MuscleGrowth Hypertrophy — mechanical tension and metabolic stress to grow muscle tissue. 65–80% 1RM, 6–15 reps, 60–90s rest. Moderate weight, noticeable pump.
ExplosivePower Primary power — maximum intent on every rep. 30–60% 1RM, 1–5 reps, 2–3 min rest. If the weight slows down, the set is done.
PowerSupport Accessory power work — moderate-intensity explosive movements that support the main power stimulus without maximal CNS demand.
Conditioning Stimuli
AerobicBase Zone 2 — easy, conversational pace. Builds mitochondria and long-term recovery capacity. If you can not hold a conversation, you are going too hard. 20–60 min steady.
Threshold Comfortably hard. Speaking is difficult but possible. Raises the pace you can sustain before oxygen debt. 10–30 min continuous or 3–10 min intervals.
VO2Max Hard intervals that expand your aerobic ceiling. 2–5 min on / 2–5 min easy. Should feel very hard by the end of each interval.
Anaerobic Maximum effort, very short bursts. 10–60 seconds all-out with 2–5× work duration rest. Trains explosive repeat capacity and lactate tolerance.
In the app

Each training day displays its stimulus as a color-coded badge — blue for MaxForce, purple for MuscleGrowth, gold for ExplosivePower, green for AerobicBase, and so on. Hybrid days (e.g. strength + aerobic) carry two badges. The slot rules for each stimulus determine which exercise slots are Required, Optional, or Forbidden on that day.

The Language of a Set
Reps, sets, rest, tempo, RPE, RIR — decoded
Foundational ▼
Sets & Reps

A rep is one complete movement. A set is a group of consecutive reps. "3×8" means 3 sets of 8 reps. More sets = more total volume = more stimulus — but beyond ~5–6 hard sets per muscle group per session, more stops producing more and just increases fatigue.

Rest Periods

Rest isn't passive — it's when your energy systems replenish so you can apply the intended stimulus on the next set.

  • MaxForce / ExplosivePower: 3–5 min. Nervous system and phosphocreatine both take time to restore.
  • MuscleGrowth: 60–90s. Shorter rest maintains the metabolic stress that contributes to growth.
  • Conditioning: Varies by stimulus — long rest (2–5× work) for Anaerobic; no rest for AerobicBase.
RPE & RIR

RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) is a 1–10 scale. RPE 10 = couldn't do another rep. RPE 7 = 3 more reps left. Most working sets should land at RPE 7–9.

RIR (Reps in Reserve) is the inverse — how many reps you had left. RIR 2 means you stopped with 2 left. Most training should be 1–3 RIR: close to failure but not grinding every set to zero.

Tempo

Written as a 4-digit code: Eccentric – Pause – Concentric – Pause. Example: 3-1-1-0 = 3 seconds lowering, 1 second pause at the bottom, 1 second pressing up. Slowing the eccentric phase dramatically increases time under tension — one of the most powerful tools for MuscleGrowth with limited equipment.

In the app

When you log a slot during a workout, you record sets, reps, load, and effort. Over time this gives you a personal performance baseline — your logged weights become the reference point for progressive overload.

Movement Patterns & Slot Types
The building blocks every exercise is made from
Foundational ▼

Every exercise is an expression of a fundamental movement pattern. Once you see exercises this way you can substitute, scale, and build programs from first principles.

🏋️ Hinge
Hip-dominant. Push hips back, load hamstrings and glutes. Deadlifts, RDLs, swings.
🦵 Squat
Knee-dominant. Deep bend with upright torso. Goblet squat, split squat, lunge.
➡️ Horizontal Push
Press parallel to floor. Bench press, push-ups. Chest, front delts, triceps.
⬆️ Vertical Push
Press overhead. Shoulder press, pike push-ups. Delts and upper traps.
↩️ Horizontal Pull
Row toward body. Bent-over rows, face pulls. Rear delts, rhomboids.
⬇️ Vertical Pull
Pull from overhead. Pull-ups, lat pulldowns. Lats and biceps.
🧱 Carry
Walk under load. Farmer carries, suitcase carries. Full-body stabilization.
🌀 Core / Anti-Rotation
Resist spinal movement. Planks, dead bugs, Pallof press. Stability first.
Slot Types

In the app, each exercise position in a training day is a slot with a typed role:

Pattern The main compound movement — Hinge, Squat, Horizontal Push, etc. Filters to matching Compound exercises.
Isolation Single-muscle accessory work. Filters by primaryMuscle (e.g. Biceps, Quads, Hamstrings, Chest).
Activation Low-load primer movements performed before the main work. Filters to Activation exercises by target joint (Hip or Shoulder) — light enough to wake up the movement pattern without creating fatigue.
Stability Core / anti-extension / anti-rotation / anti-lateral-flexion work. Filters to Stability exercises by subtype.
Power Explosive movement slot — filters to Power exercises by subtype (Jump, Sprint, Olympic Variant, Rotational).
Conditioning Energy system work — filters to Conditioning exercises by subtype (Zone2, Threshold, VO2Max, Anaerobic).
In the app

When you select an exercise for a slot, the library is already filtered to exercises that match that slot type and movement pattern — you are always training the intended movement. A balanced template has equal push and pull Pattern slots across the week. Most people have too much push and not enough pull, which is why shoulder problems are so common.

Building a Weekly Template
Days, splits, volume, and sequencing
Intermediate ▼
How Many Days?
  • 2–3 days/week: Full body each session. Hit every pattern every session. Best for beginners and limited schedules. High frequency per muscle group compensates for low total sessions.
  • 4 days/week: Upper/lower split. Train upper body twice, lower twice. Good balance of frequency and volume for most people.
  • 5 days/week: Push/pull/legs or specialised splits. Higher total volume but each muscle trained less frequently. Works well for experienced lifters needing more volume.
Stimulus Assignment

Each day in your template gets a stimulus tag that controls which slot types are Required, Optional, or Forbidden. A MuscleGrowth day will require Pattern and Isolation slots. A MaxForce day will require Pattern slots and make Isolation slots Optional. An AerobicBase day will require a Conditioning slot and forbid heavy Pattern slots.

Hybrid templates add an aerobic stimulus to certain days — e.g. MaxForce + AerobicBase — finishing a strength session with Zone 2 work. The slot rules handle both stimuli when a day carries two.

Session Sequencing

Within a session, order matters for quality of effort: highest-priority movements first, compound before isolation, Power before Strength before MuscleGrowth if mixing focuses, core and conditioning at the end.

In the app

The Plan tab shows all built-in templates filtered by Goal and days/week. Tap any template to expand its slot detail — each day's non-warmup slots are listed with their type and movement descriptor. Use the Admin tab to create a custom template with your own day structure, stimulus tags, and slot layout.

Programs & Periodization
Multi-week structure, phases, deloads, and peaks
Intermediate ▼

A weekly template repeated indefinitely works well for a while — but eventually progress stalls. Periodization is the practice of intentionally varying training over weeks and months to keep driving adaptation.

The Four Program Phases
Accumulation Build volume and work capacity. Higher reps, more sets, shorter rest. Lay the foundation — this is where muscle and aerobic base are built. Typically 3–5 weeks.
Intensification Convert what you built into strength and performance. Lower reps, higher load, longer rest. Volume decreases, intensity increases. Typically 3–4 weeks.
Realization Peak expression of what was trained. Very low reps, very high intent. Volume drops sharply to let fitness express itself. Typically 2–3 weeks.
Deload Planned 40–60% reduction in training stress. Fatigue dissipates, fitness is revealed. After a deload you should feel noticeably stronger than the week before it. 1 week.
Phase vs. Stimulus vs. Goal

A single program can mix Goals across phases — for example, the Strength + Conditioning program starts with a Strength accumulation block and finishes with a Conditioning realization block. The Phase describes where you are in the arc; the Goal describes what you are building; the Stimulus describes each day's specific demand.

In the app

Full Programs show a block timeline on each program card — color-coded chips for each phase (blue for Accumulation, purple for Intensification, gold for Realization, green for Deload) scaled proportionally by week count. Tap a program to see the full block breakdown with rep ranges, RIR targets, and the reasoning behind each block.

Modifiers — Your Toolkit
Make any exercise harder without more weight
Intermediate ▼

Modifiers change the stimulus of an exercise without changing the movement pattern. They're the answer to "I do not have more weight but I need to keep progressing."

Tempo Modifiers
  • Slow Eccentric (3–5 sec lowering): Dramatically increases time under tension. A bodyweight split squat with a 4-second lower is harder than a fast rep with 20 lbs extra.
  • Paused Reps: Stop 1–3 seconds at the bottom. Kills momentum, forces the muscle to restart from a dead stop. Good for both MaxForce and MuscleGrowth.
  • 1.5 Reps: Full rep, halfway back, drop to bottom again, all the way up. Double time in the hardest range. Brutal for quads and glutes.
  • Pulses: Short partial reps in a small range around the bottom. No full lockout — constant tension. Best for MuscleGrowth.
Isometric Modifiers
  • Iso-Holds (Yielding Isometrics): Hold still in the hardest position for 10–30 seconds. The muscle contracts maximally just to hold. Extremely effective for MuscleGrowth with no equipment.
  • Speed-Isos (Overcoming Isometrics): Push maximally against an immovable surface for 1–4 seconds. Maximum neurological recruitment. Pair immediately with explosive reps — a contrast set for ExplosivePower.
Range of Motion Modifiers
  • Deficit Variations: Stand on an elevation to increase range at the bottom. More range = more stretch = more MuscleGrowth stimulus.
  • Unilateral / Single-Limb: One leg or arm at a time. Doubles the load per limb and adds significant stability demand. Single-leg RDL, split squat, single-arm row.

Reading Your Body
Autoregulation, fatigue, soreness, and smart decisions
Advanced ▼
What Soreness Actually Means

DOMS is caused by micro-tears in muscle fiber, mostly from eccentric work. It peaks 24–48 hours after training. Soreness is a sign of novelty — not of effectiveness. Experienced lifters often feel very little soreness despite training hard. Training through mild soreness is fine; training through sharp, joint-level pain is not.

Fatigue vs. Weakness

Accumulated fatigue and actual weakness feel similar but are very different. If you feel inexplicably flat over 1–2 weeks of consistent training, it's almost certainly fatigue accumulation — your fitness is there but masked. The fix is a Deload or lighter week, not more volume.

Autoregulation in Practice

Use RPE/RIR as your guide rather than a fixed weight prescription. If your warm-up sets feel harder than usual, reduce working weight by 5–10%. Stop a set when RPE hits 9 regardless of whether you have hit your target rep count. On bad days, chase the stimulus — not the number.

In the app

Your workout history is your best autoregulation tool. If last week's MaxForce session felt like RPE 8 at a given load, and today that same load feels like an RPE 9, your history tells you to back off. Log consistently and your history becomes a map of your own recovery patterns.
